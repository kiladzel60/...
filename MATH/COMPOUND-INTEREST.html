General Information About Compound Interest

Compound interest: growth that reinvests earned interest over time

What Is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest of previous periods. This causes the investment or loan balance to grow at an increasing rate over time.

Compound interest is widely used in savings, investments, loans, and many financial instruments.

Core Formulas

Periodic Compounding

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n × t)
where P = principal, r = annual rate (decimal), n = compounding periods per year, t = years

Continuous Compounding

A = P × e^(r × t)
Example: $2,000 at 6% compounded quarterly for 5 years:
A = 2000 × (1 + 0.06/4)^(4×5) = 2000 × (1 + 0.015)^20 ≈ 2000 × 1.34885 = 2,697.70 (approx)

Effective Annual Rate (EAR)

EAR = (1 + r/n)^(n) − 1
Example: 6% compounded monthly → EAR = (1 + 0.06/12)^12 − 1 ≈ 0.061678 = 6.1678%

Solving for Unknowns

Find Principal (P)

P = A ÷ (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

Find Rate (r)

r = n × ( (A/P)^(1/(n×t)) − 1 )

Find Time (t)

t = [ ln(A/P) ] ÷ [ n × ln(1 + r/n) ]
For continuous compounding: t = ln(A/P) ÷ r

Practical Tips

  • Compare EAR: Use the Effective Annual Rate to compare different compounding schedules.
  • Frequent compounding: More frequent compounding increases the final amount for the same nominal rate.
  • Continuous compounding: Represents the theoretical limit as n → ∞ and is useful for calculus-based finance.
  • Use logs: When solving for time or rate, logarithms are required.

Real-World Applications

  • Savings accounts: Banks compound interest on deposits
  • Investments: Mutual funds and reinvested dividends compound returns
  • Loans & mortgages: Interest compounding affects total repayment amounts
  • Bonds: Certain bonds compound periodically or pay periodic coupons
